Question #94 | 2014 Past Paper | Agent_89

"Getting what one wants in life is what matters." Discuss.
The proposition that "getting what one wants in life is what matters" appears deceptively simple yet reveals profound complexities when examined closely. This materialistic worldview, which prioritizes personal desire fulfillment as life's ultimate measure of success, has gained considerable traction in our consumer-driven society. However, such a philosophy raises fundamental questions about human nature, moral responsibility, and the very definition of a meaningful existence. While personal fulfillment undoubtedly contributes to happiness, the exclusive pursuit of one's desires may ultimately prove counterproductive to both individual well-being and societal harmony.
Proponents of desire-driven living argue that personal satisfaction represents the most authentic measure of a life well-lived. After all, individuals possess unique circumstances, values, and aspirations that cannot be adequately captured by external standards of success. A person who pursues their passion for art despite societal pressure to enter a more lucrative profession may experience greater fulfillment than someone who accumulates wealth while remaining spiritually empty. Furthermore, the pursuit of personal goals often drives innovation and progress, as individuals seeking to realize their visions contribute novel solutions to human challenges. Steve Jobs's relentless pursuit of technological perfection, motivated by his personal desire to revolutionize computing, ultimately benefited millions worldwide.
However, the exclusive focus on personal desires reveals significant moral and practical limitations. Human wants are often ephemeral, contradictory, and influenced by factors beyond rational consideration. The hedonic treadmill demonstrates that achieving desired outcomes frequently fails to provide lasting satisfaction, as individuals quickly adapt to new circumstances and develop fresh desires. Moreover, unchecked pursuit of personal wants can lead to destructive behaviors, as evidenced by addiction, financial irresponsibility, and the exploitation of others for personal gain. When individual desires conflict with social responsibilities or ethical principles, the philosophy of "getting what one wants" provides insufficient guidance for navigating complex moral terrain.
Contemporary society offers numerous examples of the limitations inherent in desire-centered living. Social media platforms deliberately exploit human psychological vulnerabilities to generate engagement, creating artificial desires for validation and comparison that often result in decreased well-being. Similarly, consumer culture promotes the constant acquisition of material goods as pathways to happiness, despite mounting evidence that materialistic values correlate negatively with life satisfaction. The environmental crisis itself largely stems from humanity's collective pursuit of immediate desires without adequate consideration of long-term consequences or responsibilities to future generations.
A more balanced approach recognizes that while personal fulfillment remains important, it must be tempered by broader considerations of meaning, responsibility, and contribution to others. Research in positive psychology suggests that the most satisfied individuals combine personal achievement with service to causes greater than themselves. Viktor Frankl's experiences in Nazi concentration camps led him to conclude that meaning, rather than pleasure or desire fulfillment, constitutes the primary human motivation. This perspective suggests that the most fulfilling lives integrate personal aspirations with ethical obligations and meaningful relationships.
The concept of enlightened self-interest offers a synthesis between personal desire and broader responsibility. Intelligent pursuit of one's wants necessarily considers the social context within which individuals operate, recognizing that personal well-being depends substantially on the health of surrounding communities and institutions. A successful businessperson who prioritizes only short-term profits may ultimately undermine the economic stability that enables their success, while one who balances personal ambition with stakeholder welfare creates sustainable value for all parties involved.
In conclusion, while the pursuit of personal desires undoubtedly contributes to human motivation and satisfaction, elevating such pursuit as life's ultimate priority represents a dangerously narrow vision of human potential. The most fulfilling and sustainable approach to living integrates personal aspirations with moral responsibility, meaningful relationships, and contribution to the common good. Rather than asking merely "what do I want?" individuals might better ask "what do I want that also serves worthy purposes beyond myself?" This broader perspective acknowledges human agency while recognizing our fundamental interdependence and shared responsibility for creating a world worthy of our highest aspirations.
