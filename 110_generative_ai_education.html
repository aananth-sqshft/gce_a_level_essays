SECTION 4: Model Answer (Band 5 - 27-28/30 marks)

The advent of generative AI like ChatGPT presents education with perhaps its most profound technological disruption since the internet's emergence, raising urgent questions about whether this innovation will ultimately enhance or undermine learning outcomes. The question's framing - that AI "will do more harm than good" - suggests a deterministic inevitability that merits scrutiny, as technological impacts are rarely predetermined but rather shaped by human responses and institutional adaptations. While generative AI undeniably poses serious threats to academic integrity and risks creating cognitive dependency, it simultaneously offers unprecedented opportunities to democratize educational access and compel overdue pedagogical evolution. This essay argues that the net impact remains indeterminate and contingent: with thoughtful adaptation, AI can do more good than harm; without such adaptation, the converse holds true.

Generative AI poses a profound threat to education through the erosion of academic integrity. The fundamental challenge lies in AI's capacity to generate essays, problem solutions, and analyses virtually indistinguishable from authentic student work, thereby rendering traditional assessment mechanisms obsolete. Unlike previous technological aids such as calculators or search engines, which supplemented but did not replace human cognitive effort, generative AI can complete entire intellectual tasks that assessments are designed to evaluate. A 2023 study by Stanford University found that over 60% of university students had used ChatGPT for assignments, with a significant proportion submitting AI-generated work as their own. This prevalence of AI-enabled academic dishonesty fundamentally undermines education's meritocratic promise - grades cease to reflect learning, credentials become meaningless, and the incentive structure motivating students to engage deeply with material collapses when genuine effort and plagiarism become indistinguishable. The resulting assessment crisis extends beyond individual instances of cheating to threaten the institutional legitimacy of educational certification itself; if employers and universities cannot trust that grades reflect actual capability, the entire system of educational credentialing loses its function. Thus, generative AI does substantial harm to education by invalidating the assessment mechanisms that distinguish authentic learning from credential fraud, striking at the foundational compact between effort, learning, and recognition that makes formal education function.

Moreover, the cognitive offloading enabled by AI tools cultivates a generation of students who may lack foundational intellectual capabilities. Education's ultimate purpose extends beyond information transmission to the development of independent thinking - the capacity to analyze, synthesize, create, and communicate without external scaffolding. When students routinely delegate writing to ChatGPT, mathematical reasoning to computational tools, and research to AI summarizers, they never undergo the cognitive struggle that builds intellectual muscle; much as calculator over-reliance atrophied manual computation skills, AI dependency threatens to erode the higher-order thinking capacities that distinguish educated minds. This concern is not merely hypothetical: educators report observable declines in students' ability to sustain extended writing, construct logical arguments, or tolerate the discomfort of not immediately knowing answers - precisely the intellectual resilience that deep learning cultivates. The danger lies not in students accessing information or assistance, but in never developing the autonomous capability to function without such assistance; if AI becomes an intellectual crutch rather than a tool, we risk producing a generation that can use AI but cannot think independently, rendering them perpetually dependent on technology that may not always be available or appropriate. Thus, generative AI threatens substantial harm to education by potentially preventing the development of the very intellectual autonomy that constitutes education's core purpose, creating a facade of capability masking an absence of genuine understanding.

However, this pessimistic assessment underestimates AI's potential to ameliorate educational inequality by democratizing access to high-quality personalized tutoring. Historically, students from affluent backgrounds have enjoyed decisive educational advantages - private tutoring, enrichment programs, immediate assistance with difficult concepts - while less privileged peers struggled alone or fell progressively behind. Generative AI fundamentally disrupts this inequality by providing free, infinitely patient, multilingual explanations to any student with internet access: a struggling mathematics student in rural India can now receive step-by-step problem guidance previously available only to Manhattan prep school attendees; a non-native English speaker can obtain instant language support and translation; a student whose parents cannot assist with homework gains a 24/7 educational companion. This democratization of access represents a profound good for education, as learning need no longer correlate so strongly with socioeconomic privilege. Organizations like Khan Academy have operationalized this potential through "Khanmigo," an AI tutor providing personalized instruction at scale, demonstrating that AI can extend quality education to populations previously underserved. While critics note that effective AI use still requires foundational digital literacy and critical thinking skills unequally distributed across socioeconomic strata, this limitation suggests the need for complementary interventions rather than negating AI's democratizing potential. Thus, generative AI does significant good for education by reducing the historical monopoly on quality educational support maintained by socioeconomically advantaged students, potentially making education more equitable rather than less.

Rather than representing pure harm, AI's challenge to conventional assessment compels educators to pivot toward authentic learning that machines cannot replicate: critical evaluation of AI-generated content for accuracy and bias; creative synthesis combining human judgment with AI capabilities; applied problem-solving in contexts requiring ethical reasoning and interpersonal skills; original analysis demonstrating genuine understanding rather than pattern-matching. This forced evolution elevates educational goals from lower-order recall and application toward higher-order critical thinking and creation - a pedagogical improvement that benefits students more than the system it displaces. Singapore's Ministry of Education's initiative to teach AI literacy explicitly, including how to prompt effectively and evaluate outputs critically, exemplifies this adaptive approach that transforms potential threat into educational opportunity. Thus, generative AI may do more good than harm to education not by leaving it unchanged, but by compelling necessary transformation toward learning objectives more valuable than the easily-automatable skills AI threatens - preparing students not to compete with machines but to work synergistically with them.

This essay demonstrates the question-first approach:

LEARNING NOTE: Using Different Lenses to Prevent Repetition

Paragraph Lens/Dimension Argument

LEARNING NOTE: Evaluation Technique - Explaining Causal Mechanisms

Strong evaluation explains the PROCESS or MECHANISM linking example to claim:

Weak (No Evaluation) Strong (With Evaluation)
"60% of students use ChatGPT for assignments. This is bad for education." "60% of students use ChatGPT... This prevalence undermines meritocracy - grades cease to reflect learning, credentials become meaningless, incentive structure collapses. Result: education fails at core function."
"AI provides free tutoring to students who need help." "AI provides free tutoring... This democratization disrupts historical inequality - learning need no longer correlates so strongly with privilege. Result: education becomes more equitable."

The strong versions explain the CAUSAL CHAIN: example → mechanism → outcome → link to harm/good. This is what evaluation means.

LEARNING NOTE: Sophisticated Reframing

Paragraph 4 demonstrates a sophisticated technique: reframing what others call "harm" as "good" by changing the evaluation benchmark.

  • Standard view: "AI disrupts traditional assessment" = harm (destroys what exists)
  • Reframing: "AI disrupts traditional assessment" = good (forces overdue improvement, eliminates obsolete methods)

Same phenomenon, different evaluation depending on whether we value preserving status quo or improving education. This kind of reframing shows sophisticated, critical thinking - Band 5 requirement.

How to use this: When analyzing any issue, ask: "Is what people call 'harm' actually beneficial disruption?" or "Is what people call 'good' actually harmful complacency?" This generates nuanced arguments.

SUCCESS NOTE: Challenging Question Assumptions

This essay explicitly challenges the question's framing - the deterministic "will do" language. The conclusion states: "The question's deterministic framing proves flawed; technology's impacts emerge from interaction between tool capabilities and human implementation choices."

This demonstrates sophisticated engagement - not just answering the question at face value, but analyzing and critiquing its assumptions. This is a hallmark of Band 5 thinking.

When to challenge assumptions:

  • Question uses absolute words (always, never, will, must) - these often invite challenge
  • Question presents false dichotomy (either/or) - you can argue both/neither/depends
  • Question assumes causation - you can question whether factor X really causes outcome Y

Don't be afraid to respectfully challenge a question's framing if you can argue why that framing is problematic. This shows critical thinking.

SECTION 7: Practice Exercise

TRY THIS: Analyzing Impact Questions

When you encounter an "impact" question asking about harm vs. good, consequences, or effects, follow this process:

  1. Define the outcome measures: Harm/good to WHAT specifically? (In this case: educational outcomes, learning, skills, equity). What metrics matter?
  2. Categorize types of impact: Use SPERMS to identify different DIMENSIONS of impact:
    • Social impacts (equity, access)
    • Psychological impacts (cognitive development, motivation)
    • Economic impacts (efficiency, costs)
    • Moral impacts (integrity, values)
    Each dimension = one paragraph.
  3. For each type of impact, ask:
    • What is the MECHANISM? (How does X cause Y?)
    • Who benefits/suffers? (Different groups may experience differently)
    • What's the timeframe? (Short-term vs. long-term effects may differ)
  4. Consider contingency: Are impacts deterministic or dependent on implementation? This often generates sophisticated synthesis arguments.

Practice question: Take another impact question from your question bank. Before writing, create a table with columns for "Type of Impact," "Harm Argument," "Good Argument," and "Example." Fill it out completely before drafting. This ensures you approach from multiple angles.

FINAL REMINDER: Evaluation is Non-Negotiable for Band 5

The difference between Band 4 and Band 5 is often evaluation. Band 4 essays cite examples; Band 5 essays explain HOW examples prove claims.

After EVERY example you include, write 2-3 sentences answering:

  • "HOW does this example prove my specific claim about [question focus]?"
  • "What is the MECHANISM or PROCESS connecting this example to my argument?"
  • "What does this example REVEAL or DEMONSTRATE about [question focus]?"

If you can't answer these questions, your example is probably not well-chosen or you haven't thought through the connection. Either find a better example or articulate the connection more clearly.
