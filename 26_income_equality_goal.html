Question #26: To what extent should income equality be a goal in your society?

Model Answer: 789 words | Target Band: 5

SECTION 1: Understanding the Question

Question Type & Analysis

  • Type (VIPERCJ): VALUE question - asking about importance and desirability of income equality as societal goal
  • Core Issue: Should Singapore actively pursue income equality, or should market inequality be accepted as necessary incentive mechanism?
  • Singapore Context - CRITICAL: Singapore's meritocratic ideology claims equal opportunity rewards different efforts unequally—accepting inequality as natural outcome. Yet rising Gini coefficient (inequality metric) from 0.42 (2000) to 0.458 (2019) reveals growing disparity. The tension: meritocratic rhetoric vs inequality reality; government compassion claims vs minimal redistributive policy.
UNDERSTANDING "SHOULD BE A GOAL":

This asks about desirability and intention—should Singapore actively pursue income equality through policy, or is present inequality acceptable/necessary? The question implies choice: equality is not inevitable but depends on what society deliberately aims for.

SECTION 2: Framework Analysis

Element Analysis
Topic Desirability of income equality; balance between inequality incentive and social cohesion costs
Issue Singapore claims meritocracy justifies inequality but fails to address inequality's actual sources (inherited advantage, skill-biased growth, foreign worker exploitation)
Opinion Income equality SHOULD be a priority goal in Singapore, not through total equalization but through: (1) genuine equality of opportunity addressing inherited disadvantage, (2) redistribution addressing market-generated inequality, (3) dignified minimum standards. Current Singapore approach (market-driven inequality with minimal intervention) fails both meritocratic and compassionate values it claims.
Evidence Dimensions Economic (Gini coefficient, wage gaps), Social (social cohesion, health inequality), Meritocratic (inequality of opportunity), Political (policy choices, alternatives)

SECTION 3: Model Answer

Income equality should be a significant societal goal in Singapore, not through unrealistic income leveling but through deliberate policy reducing inequality's most damaging dimensions. Singapore's meritocratic ideology claims to justify inequality as fair outcome of differential talent and effort; however, extensive research reveals that inherited advantage, structural inequality, and market failures undermine meritocratic logic. An income equality goal would require Singapore to address whether current inequality reflects genuine meritocracy (fair) or systemic disadvantage (unjust)—a question the nation currently evades through minimal redistributive policy.

Singapore's growing income inequality reveals meritocracy's failure and undermines social cohesion. The Gini coefficient has risen from 0.42 in 2000 to 0.458 in 2019, placing Singapore among most unequal developed nations. More significantly, intergenerational mobility has declined: children born to low-income parents have increasingly constrained prospects regardless of talent, revealing that inequality increasingly correlates with inherited disadvantage rather than individual merit. The wage gap between top and bottom earners has widened dramatically—a CEO-to-janitor ratio of 250:1 reflects not pure meritocracy but structural inequality. Foreign workers in precarious low-wage sectors experience systematic wage suppression; when employers can import cheaper labor, domestic workers' wages are depressed, making inequality a policy choice (allowing worker importation) rather than market inevitability. Therefore, Singapore's inequality fails meritocratic justification: it reflects inherited advantage and structural disadvantage, not differential talent.

Income equality should be a goal because inequality undermines the social cohesion Singapore depends upon. Research consistently shows that high-inequality societies experience reduced social trust, increased health disparities across income groups, and lower life satisfaction even among wealthy citizens. Singapore's multicultural society is particularly vulnerable: when inequality becomes racialized (certain ethnic groups concentrated in low-wage work due to structural barriers), inequality threatens social harmony. The 2016 Little India riot partially reflected frustrations among migrant workers facing systematic economic marginalization—illustrating how inequality creates social fracture. Additionally, Singapore's aging population requires enhanced social welfare; extreme inequality means many elderly cannot afford healthcare or dignified living, requiring government rescue of the market-failed population. Thus, income equality is not abstract fairness but practical necessity for social stability and cohesion.

Income equality need not mean identical incomes; rather, Singapore should pursue three concrete equality goals: First, genuine equality of opportunity: providing low-income children with quality education, healthcare, and early childhood development equal to wealthy children. Currently, ZIP code predicts outcomes—public school quality varies dramatically by neighborhood, contradicting meritocratic principle. Second, income floor protecting dignity: Singapore's minimum wage remains below poverty line; a $1,500 monthly minimum would enable dignified living while maintaining strong incentive differentials (CEO earning 50 times more than minimum wage—still enormous gap but compatible with equality goal). Third, progressive taxation and redistribution: Singapore's tax rate (effective 5%) is among world's lowest, meaning public revenue inadequate for social investment. Moderately progressive taxation (not punitive) would enable healthcare, education, and elder care investments. These specific goals pursue equality in meaningful dimensions without requiring income leveling.

However, income equality pursuit faces genuine tensions: extreme equality can reduce productivity incentives; Singapore's approach prioritizes growth, viewing inequality as necessary. Yet this framing false-chooses between growth and equality—countries like Denmark and Finland maintain strong growth with far greater equality. Moreover, Singapore's inequality arguably reduces growth by constraining lower-income population's consumption and human capital development. Thus, growth and equality are not incompatible; Singapore's present trajectory simply prioritizes inequality-generating policies.

Ultimately, income equality should be a central goal for Singapore because: current inequality fails to reflect meritocratic justice (reflects inherited advantage), undermines social cohesion (especially critical for multicultural nation), and creates dignified living challenges (aging population, precarious workers). Singapore's meritocratic ideology itself demands equality pursuit: if society claims to reward talent fairly, it must address how inherited advantage and structural barriers undermine fair competition. The question is not whether Singapore can afford equality goals, but whether it will acknowledge that present inequality-accepting approach contradicts both the meritocratic and compassionate values upon which Singapore's social legitimacy depends.

SECTION 4: Key Learning Points

LEARNING POINT #1 - Reframing "Should Be a Goal" to Show Knowledge of Alternatives:

Instead of generic "equality is good," the essay acknowledges opposing view (growth requires inequality incentive) then shows why it's false choice. This demonstrates critical thinking by engaging with strongest counterargument, not strawman.

LEARNING POINT #2 - Using Data to Undermine Meritocratic Narrative:

Gini coefficient (0.42→0.458), CEO-to-janitor ratios, intergenerational mobility decline—these stats show inequality isn't meritocratic outcome but structural. Using data this way prevents abstract claims and grounds argument in Singapore reality.

LEARNING POINT #3 - Operationalizing Abstract Concepts (Equality Goals):

Rather than vague "pursue equality," the essay specifies: (1) equal opportunity, (2) income floor, (3) progressive taxation. This concreteness is critical—it shows you understand what "goal" actually means in policy terms, elevating response from theoretical to practical.

LEARNING POINT #4 - Singapore-Specific Contextualization:

The essay connects inequality to multicultural harmony, aging population, specific wage gaps. This prevents generic essay about equality by grounding every claim in Singapore's particular vulnerabilities and values. Always ask: "Why does THIS society need equality?"
