Model Answer Analysis: Scientific Research Access

Question #7 | Type: Justification (J) | Topic: Science & Technology | Global Scope

Question:

"'The results of scientific research should be available to everyone.' How far do you agree?"

SECTION 1: Initial Thought Process & Approach

Step 1: First Impressions

  • Question type: JUSTIFICATION (J) – asking whether it is justified/right that research results should be available to everyone. Not describing what IS, but asking about what SHOULD be.
  • Key word: "Everyone" – This is broad and potentially idealistic. "Everyone" includes developing countries, individual researchers with no funds, people in restricted regimes. The word signals: this is an absolute position worth examining.
  • Two sides emerge immediately:
    • FOR: Research should be free/open access (benefits humanity, knowledge is public good, taxpayer-funded research, promotes global equity)
    • AGAINST: Some restrictions justified (incentivizes research, protects intellectual property, security concerns, commercialization funds future research)
  • Core tension: Individual/corporate rights to profit from research vs. collective human benefit from accessible knowledge
CRITICAL ANNOTATION: Non-Singapore Question

Notice: This question does NOT ask about "your society." It's a global question about scientific research universally. This means:

  • Don't force Singapore examples artificially
  • Use SPERMS, VIPERCJ, and general frameworks instead of AAME
  • Draw examples from global science context
  • If you mention Singapore, do so naturally (e.g., Singapore's biotech research, Singapore scientists' access needs)

Many students make the mistake of forcing "Singapore context" into non-Singapore questions. This reduces relevance. Answer the actual question asked—if it's global, answer globally.

Step 2: Keyword Analysis

  • "Results of scientific research": The end products—published papers, data, findings. Not necessarily the process or methodologies, but the knowledge gained.
  • "Should be available": Normative question—whether it's ethically right/justified. Implies current state may not match ideal.
  • "To everyone": Universal access—no barriers of cost, geography, institution, or clearance. This is radical; even advocates rarely claim literally EVERYONE should access all research (e.g., weapons research).

Step 3: Conceptual Framing

This question is fundamentally about: Who owns knowledge? Is scientific knowledge a public good or a private commodity?

The underlying tensions:

  • Idealism vs. Pragmatism: Ideally all knowledge should be free; practically, research needs funding
  • Public good vs. Private property: Knowledge is humanity's heritage vs. knowledge is asset worthy of protection
  • Equity vs. Incentive: Open access benefits poor researchers globally vs. restricted access funds innovation

SECTION 3: Band 5 Model Answer (770 words)

INTRODUCTION

The claim that scientific research results should be available to everyone raises a fundamental question about knowledge ownership and global equity. While the ideal of universal, free access to research appears intuitively just—enabling global scientists to benefit from and build upon discoveries—the practical reality is more nuanced. I agree that research results SHOULD be more widely available than current paywalled systems allow, but I argue that complete universal free access for all research is neither realistic nor entirely justified, as security concerns, intellectual property incentives, and practical constraints necessitate some restrictions while we expand open access wherever possible.

Introduction Analysis:
Position clarity: Not absolute yes/no, but nuanced: "more available than current" but "not completely free for all research." This signals sophisticated thinking.
Acknowledgment of both sides: Opens with the appeal of universal access before complicating it with practical considerations.
Link to question: Uses question keywords "available," "everyone," "results," and clearly states stance.

BODY PARAGRAPH 1 (FOR Side - Equity and Global Benefit)

The most compelling argument for making research available to everyone is the equity dimension: current paywalled publishing systems systematically exclude scientists in developing nations, reinforcing global research inequality. When a groundbreaking medical study is published behind a $30+ paywall accessible only to wealthy universities, researchers in Africa, India, and Southeast Asia cannot access it, meaning brilliant scientists are denied the knowledge necessary to advance their own work. The consequences are tangible: during the pandemic, open access to COVID-19 research accelerated vaccine development globally, while paywalled research on less profitable diseases (malaria, dengue fever, affecting primarily poor populations) progresses slowly because developing-nation researchers cannot access prior research. This disparity demonstrates that restricted access literally costs lives and perpetuates the pattern of wealthy nations advancing science while poorer nations lag behind. Furthermore, much research is publicly funded through government grants: taxpayers in developed nations already paid for the research through their taxes, yet they cannot access the published results without paying publishers again. This is logically indefensible—the public funded it; the public should access it. Thus, from equity and public funding perspectives, research results SHOULD be more available globally to eliminate barriers that currently exclude capable scientists from accessing knowledge.

Paragraph 1 Analysis:
Different angle from eventual counter-argument: Para 1 focuses on EQUITY/SOCIAL dimension. Later paragraphs will address ECONOMIC and SECURITY dimensions, ensuring different arguments.
Example evaluation: "This disparity demonstrates..." connects COVID/malaria example to broader point (systemic inequality). Doesn't just state example; explains why it matters.
Concrete data: $30+ paywall price is specific. COVID example is timely and relevant. Malaria/dengue show real consequences.
Logic-based argument: "Taxpayers paid; should access" is a straightforward logical argument, not dependent on memorized facts.

BODY PARAGRAPH 2 (FOR Side - Scientific Progress Acceleration)

Beyond equity, open access to research results directly accelerates scientific progress itself. Science advances through incremental building—researchers read existing work, identify gaps, and build upon foundations. When foundational research is paywalled, this process halts for researchers without access. Studies on open access research show it receives significantly more citations and has greater research impact than paywalled equivalents, suggesting that greater availability actually enhances scientific progress. The mechanism is straightforward: more researchers accessing research = more people building upon it = faster innovation. Consider artificial intelligence development: the rapid advances in AI over the past decade correlate directly with open access to foundational papers and datasets. Researchers from different nations, institutions, and backgrounds accessed the same papers simultaneously, each contributing improvements, creating exponential acceleration. By contrast, proprietary AI research held by corporations advances more slowly because fewer minds can access and build upon it. If all research operated under proprietary restrictions, science would essentially slow down, as knowledge would be fragmented across institutions unable to access each other's work. Therefore, making research available to everyone is justified not just ethically but pragmatically—it accelerates scientific progress that benefits all of humanity.

Paragraph 2 Analysis:
Different argument than Para 1: Para 1 was about equity. Para 2 is about scientific efficiency/progress. Both favor open access but from different angles.
Mechanism explanation: "more researchers accessing = more building upon = faster innovation" explains the causal chain.
Evidence quality: Studies showing citations are mentioned specifically; AI example is contemporary and illustrative.
Evaluation of AI example: Not just "AI advanced" but explained why: "open access allows more minds to contribute."

BODY PARAGRAPH 3 (AGAINST Side - Intellectual Property and Research Funding)

However, the argument for complete universal free access overlooks the practical role of intellectual property protection in funding research. Developing new drugs costs pharmaceutical companies $1-3 billion and takes 10-15 years; without patent protection allowing them to recoup costs through exclusive sale rights, companies simply would not fund drug development. Remove patent protection = remove incentive for companies to invest billions = less research conducted. This is not mere theory; pharmaceutical innovation has demonstrably decreased in therapeutic areas without patent protection or public funding, such as antibiotics (less profitable, so corporations abandon research despite urgent need). Furthermore, much research is conducted by commercial entities and startups whose business model depends on proprietary knowledge: software companies need intellectual property protection to justify the investment in developing new technologies. If all results were immediately freely available, the competitive advantage of investing in research disappears. Admittedly, this creates tension with equity goals; the solution is nuanced: publicly funded research should generally be open access, while privately funded proprietary research might justify temporary restriction, or there should be mechanisms (compulsory licensing, forced generic production during emergencies) that balance profit incentives with access needs. Thus, while the ideal of universal free availability is appealing, intellectual property rights play a legitimate functional role in incentivizing research, suggesting some restrictions on availability are justified.

Paragraph 3 Analysis:
Shift to counter-argument: Acknowledges merits of opposite view while explaining limitations of absolute open access.
Specific evidence: $1-3 billion drug development cost is concrete; 10-15 years timeframe is specific; antibiotic example shows real consequences of removing incentive.
Qualifier admission: "Admittedly, this creates tension..." shows balanced thinking; doesn't dismiss equity concerns while arguing for property rights.
Nuanced solution: Rather than choosing sides, suggests compromise (public research open, private proprietary but with emergency provisions). This is sophisticated.
Different dimension: Para 1 & 2 used SOCIAL/EQUITY dimension. Para 3 uses ECONOMIC dimension. Different lenses prevent repetition.

BODY PARAGRAPH 4 (AGAINST Side - Security and Practical Constraints)

Additionally, some research legitimately requires restricted access for security and safety reasons. Dual-use research—research with both civilian and military applications—cannot ethically be made universally available. For example, detailed research on pathogenic engineering could enable bioterrorists to weaponize diseases; making such research available to everyone would expose population to preventable catastrophic risk. Similarly, research on cryptography-breaking or cybersecurity vulnerabilities requires careful dissemination to prevent criminals from exploiting disclosed weaknesses before defenses can be implemented. In these cases, restricted access is not about profit incentive but about public safety—making such research available to literally everyone would cause direct harm. Furthermore, truly universal access faces practical constraints: some research involves proprietary equipment or requires specialized training to use safely. A laboratory procedure for handling dangerous pathogens should not be "available to everyone" in the sense of accessible to any untrained person; it requires qualified training. These practical limitations suggest that "available to everyone" is an aspirational but not realistic standard for all research. Therefore, while expanding access to research is desirable, complete universal availability across all research is neither justifiable (security concerns) nor practical (training requirements, equipment access).

Paragraph 4 Analysis:
New dimension: Para 3 was ECONOMIC. Para 4 is SECURITY/PRACTICAL. Another different lens.
Concrete examples: Bioterror, cryptography, lab safety—specific real concerns, not abstract.
Mechanism explanation: "Restricted access prevents harm" and "practical training requirements exist" are different justifications for restriction.
Avoiding absolute positions: Doesn't say all research needs restriction, just that some legitimately does.
Four-paragraph structure gives four different angles: Para 1 = Equity, Para 2 = Scientific Progress, Para 3 = Economic Incentive, Para 4 = Security/Practical. No repetition.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, while scientific research results should ideally be available widely, the question's assumption that they should be available to everyone universally is more complicated than the absolutist framing suggests. The compelling arguments for expanded access—equity, accelerated progress, justice for taxpayer-funded research—are genuinely weighty and should drive policy toward open access as the default. However, legitimate justifications exist for some restrictions: intellectual property protection that funds research, security concerns that prevent weaponization, and practical constraints of training and equipment. The most defensible position is not all-or-nothing but contextual: publicly funded research should be openly accessible (taxpayers paid, deserve access); research with security implications should remain restricted; commercially funded research might justify temporary proprietary protection before eventual open access; and practical safety/training requirements should be respected. Therefore, I agree that research results SHOULD be more widely available than current paywalled systems allow, and open access should be the default where possible, but the absolute claim that all results should be available to literally everyone is neither realistic nor entirely justified given legitimate counterarguments.

Conclusion Analysis:
Nuanced synthesis: Doesn't choose between sides but creates a framework (contextual approach) that accommodates both.
Multi-factor consideration: Addresses equity, funding, security, and practicality—comprehensive synthesis.
Final stance is clear: Agrees with general direction (more access) but rejects absolutism (not to literally everyone).
Links back to question: Uses question keywords "available," "everyone," and clearly states qualified agreement/disagreement.

Key Learning: Nuanced Positions in "How Far Do You Agree" Questions

This essay demonstrates that "How far do you agree" questions don't require binary yes/no answers. The most sophisticated responses often take positions like:

  • "I agree with the general principle, but with important caveats..."
  • "I agree in some contexts but not others..."
  • "The ideal is correct, but practical limitations create exceptions..."

This essay takes this approach: "Agree that research SHOULD be more available, but disagree that it should be universally available to literally everyone."

This demonstrates Band 5 thinking because it shows:

  • Nuanced understanding (not black/white)
  • Acknowledgment of multiple legitimate perspectives
  • Ability to propose contextual solutions rather than absolute rules
