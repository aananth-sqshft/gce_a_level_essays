Is globalisation to be welcomed or feared today?
Question #33 • 2019 Past Paper • Economics • Value Question
Agent_30 • Generated: November 2025

In our interconnected world of 2025, globalisation stands as one of the most defining forces shaping human civilisation. From the smartphone assembled across multiple continents to the instantaneous flow of capital that can destabilise entire economies, globalisation's reach extends into every facet of modern life. Yet this phenomenon presents a paradox: while it has delivered unprecedented prosperity and connectivity, it has simultaneously generated new vulnerabilities and inequalities that challenge the very foundations of national sovereignty and social cohesion. The question of whether to welcome or fear globalisation cannot be answered with simple binary thinking; instead, it demands a nuanced examination of its multifaceted impacts across economic, social, and political dimensions.

The economic case for welcoming globalisation remains compelling, particularly when viewed through the lens of aggregate global prosperity. The integration of global markets has facilitated the most dramatic reduction in absolute poverty in human history, lifting over one billion people out of extreme destitution since 1990. Countries like China and India, through their strategic engagement with global trade networks, have transformed from predominantly agrarian societies into major economic powerhouses. This transformation has not occurred in isolation; it has created a ripple effect of opportunity, enabling developing nations to leverage their comparative advantages in labour-intensive industries while providing consumers worldwide with access to affordable goods and services. The efficiency gains from global supply chains have made everything from clothing to electronics more accessible to ordinary families, effectively democratising consumption in ways that would have been impossible under autarkic economic models.

Furthermore, globalisation has accelerated the pace of innovation and knowledge transfer in unprecedented ways. The collaborative development of COVID-19 vaccines, achieved in record time through international scientific cooperation, exemplifies how globalisation can mobilise human ingenuity to address collective challenges. Similarly, the rapid dissemination of digital technologies and best practices across borders has enabled developing countries to leapfrog traditional development stages, with nations like Kenya pioneering mobile banking solutions that are now being adopted in developed economies. This cross-pollination of ideas and technologies represents one of globalisation's most valuable contributions to human progress.

However, the darker dimensions of globalisation present legitimate grounds for apprehension. The 2008 financial crisis starkly illustrated how interconnected markets can transform localised problems into global catastrophes, with the collapse of American subprime mortgages triggering a worldwide recession that devastated economies far removed from Wall Street. More recently, the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of global supply chains, as disruptions in manufacturing hubs created shortages of essential goods worldwide. These vulnerabilities highlight how globalisation can amplify systemic risks, creating new categories of threats that no single nation can adequately address through unilateral action.

Perhaps more troubling is globalisation's role in exacerbating inequality within nations, even as it has reduced inequality between them. In developed countries, the offshoring of manufacturing jobs has hollowed out middle-class employment opportunities, contributing to the political polarisation evident in Brexit, the rise of populist movements, and growing skepticism toward international institutions. The benefits of globalisation have been disproportionately captured by mobile capital and highly skilled workers, while those dependent on location-specific employment have borne the costs of adjustment. This has created a dangerous political dynamic where the losers from globalisation increasingly view international integration as a zero-sum game that benefits distant elites at their expense.

The environmental implications of globalisation present another source of legitimate concern. The current model of global trade, dependent on carbon-intensive transportation and the race-to-the-bottom in environmental standards, has accelerated climate change and environmental degradation. The pursuit of cost efficiency has often meant production shifting to countries with the weakest environmental protections, creating a "pollution haven" effect that undermines global sustainability efforts.

In navigating these complexities, the appropriate response is neither uncritical embrace nor wholesale rejection of globalisation, but rather its intelligent management and reform. This requires strengthening international institutions to better regulate global markets, ensuring that the benefits of integration are more widely shared while building resilience against systemic risks. Countries must invest in education and social safety nets to help their populations adapt to rapid economic change, while international cooperation on tax policy can prevent the erosion of public resources needed for these investments.

Ultimately, globalisation in 2025 should be cautiously welcomed, but only with the recognition that its current trajectory requires significant course corrections. The interconnectedness that enables rapid vaccine development and lifts millions from poverty is the same force that can spread financial contagion and accelerate environmental destruction. The challenge lies not in choosing between globalisation and isolationism, but in crafting a more sustainable and equitable form of international integration—one that harnesses the creative potential of human cooperation while building safeguards against its destructive excesses. In this balance between opportunity and vulnerability lies the key to a globalisation that truly serves human flourishing.
