SECTION 3: Model Answer (738 words)

Introduction

The question of whether countries should maintain diplomatic and economic relations with human rights violators presents a genuine moral dilemma between two competing values: the pragmatic principle that engagement may drive positive change, and the ethical conviction that such relations implicitly condone abuses. How far countries should maintain such relations depends on balancing the likelihood that engagement produces substantive human rights improvements against the cost of appearing to legitimize systematic violations. This essay will examine both the practical arguments for selective engagement and the principled arguments against normalization, concluding that limited, conditional relations—coupled with explicit criticism and sanctions for specific violations—constitute the most defensible approach.

Paragraph 1: The Case for Strategic Engagement

The argument for maintaining relations with human rights violators rests on the pragmatic observation that diplomatic and economic isolation rarely improves human rights records, while engagement through dialogue creates opportunities for influence. China's gradual opening to foreign investment, despite ongoing human rights concerns, has produced both economic development that enables civil society participation and certain limited improvements in living standards and education access—benefits that absolute isolation would not have achieved. Furthermore, complete economic isolation often strengthens authoritarian regimes by allowing them to blame external enemies for internal hardship, consolidating nationalist support and reducing pressure for internal reform. Conversely, countries like Vietnam and Laos have experienced marginal human rights improvements through international trade relationships and exposure to global norms, suggesting that engagement creates channels for influence that isolation forecloses. Thus, countries may justifiably maintain relations with poor human rights records when such relations offer realistic prospects for incremental improvement through dialogue and economic incentives.

Paragraph 2: Strategic Conditionality

However, meaningful engagement is not unconditional: countries can maintain relations while explicitly conditioning advanced benefits on human rights improvement. The EU's approach to Turkey exemplifies this model: maintaining diplomatic relations while threatening exclusion from trade benefits and institutional participation unless specific human rights concerns (press freedom, minority rights) are addressed. This conditionality strategy maintains dialogue channels while signaling that violations have consequences. Without conditionality, relations become indistinguishable from normalization, suggesting that systematic abuse carries no diplomatic cost. Thus, strategic engagement must include explicit consequences for violations to remain morally defensible.

Paragraph 3: The Principle Argument Against Relations

However, a significant counterargument contends that maintaining relations inherently legitimizes human rights violations, regardless of strategic intentions. When democracies engage in normal diplomatic relations with authoritarian states, this implies acceptance of their internal governance—a troubling moral concession. Myanmar's military junta, despite international criticism, derives legitimacy from maintained relations with key trading partners; engagement without genuine leverage appears to enable rather than constrain abuses. Furthermore, the opportunity costs of engagement are often overlooked: resources spent dialoguing with persistent violators could instead support civil society and human rights defenders within those societies. Thus, countries must weigh whether maintaining relations serves human rights interests or merely provides diplomatic convenience disguised as pragmatism.

Paragraph 4: The Effectiveness Challenge

The critical question remains: does engagement actually improve human rights records, or does it merely provide regimes with diplomatic legitimacy while violations continue? Evidence suggests mixed results. Saudi Arabia maintains robust international relations while committing systematic human rights abuses; Russian relations with Western countries have not produced improvements in journalist safety or minority rights. This suggests that countries have insufficient leverage through engagement alone to produce meaningful human rights improvement. A more sophisticated approach recognizes that different situations warrant different strategies: with partners showing progress (Vietnam), engagement may be justified; with unchanging violators (North Korea, Myanmar's junta), isolation might be more principled. Thus, how far countries should maintain relations depends on honest assessment of whether engagement actually produces human rights improvement or merely provides diplomatic comfort.

Conclusion

The defensible position is not absolute isolation nor unconditional engagement, but rather differentiated relations calibrated to actual human rights trajectories. Countries should maintain dialogue and limited relations with rights violators showing incremental improvement while explicitly conditioning advanced benefits (trade partnerships, institutional participation, diplomatic recognition) on specific human rights commitments. For regimes showing no progress despite decades of engagement (Myanmar, North Korea, Syria), isolation becomes more defensible as a principled stance. The key is preventing engagement from becoming an excuse for inaction: relations maintained without explicit consequences for violations become morally indefensible normalization of abuse. Thus, countries can justifiably maintain some relations with poor human rights records when those relations are explicitly conditional, strategically focused on demonstrable improvement, and coupled with active support for human rights defenders within those societies.
